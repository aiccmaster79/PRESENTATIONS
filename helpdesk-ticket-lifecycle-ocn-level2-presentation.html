Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Map the stages of a helpdesk ticket
  • Write clear ticket notes that another technician can follow
  • Prioritise using impact and urgency ideas
  • Escalate appropriately when stuck
  • Close tickets only after user confirmation

Tickets Are the Story of Support

A ticket is not bureaucracy — it is the shared record of a problem, the work done, and the outcome. Good tickets protect users and technicians.

Lifecycle Stages

  1. Report — user submits portal form, email, or phone call
  2. Log — create ticket with contact, asset, and symptom details
  3. Categorise & prioritise — type, impact, urgency
  4. Investigate & resolve — diagnose, fix, or workaround
  5. Communicate — keep the user informed of progress
  6. Confirm & close — user accepts; add resolution notes / KB link

What Goes in a Good Ticket

Who

Name, contact, location/asset tag

What

Symptom in user’s words + your restatement

When

Started, frequency, recent changes

Tried

Steps already attempted

Actions

What you did, in order

Outcome

Fixed / workaround / escalated

Test Your Knowledge!

Let's see what you've learned about the ticket lifecycle

Q1: You should close a ticket when…

AYou feel bored
BThe issue is resolved and the user confirms (or policy allows auto-close)
CYou have sent any reply at all
DThe user is on lunch
Answer: B — Closure needs resolution evidence and confirmation rules.

Q2: Escalation is appropriate when…

AYou want someone else to do easy work
BThe issue exceeds your access/skills or breaches SLA risk
CThe ticket title is short
DIt is raining
Answer: B — Escalate with context — don’t dump empty tickets.

Q3: A high-quality ticket note should…

ASay only “fixed”
BLet another technician understand symptoms, actions and outcome
CInclude the user’s passwords
DBe written in unexplained slang only
Answer: B — Notes are for the next human — clarity wins.

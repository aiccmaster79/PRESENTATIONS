What is 5G?

Definition

5G is the fifth generation of mobile network technology. It delivers much faster speeds, lower delay, and the ability to connect far more devices than previous generations.

Think of it like this...

If 4G is a busy dual carriageway, 5G is a multi-lane motorway—more vehicles (devices), moving faster, with less waiting at junctions (latency).

4G vs 5G

4G LTE

  • Typical speeds: 10–50 Mbps
  • Latency around 30–50 ms
  • Designed mainly for smartphones
  • Good for streaming & browsing
  • Limited device density
  • Widely available today

5G

  • Peak speeds: 1–10+ Gbps
  • Latency as low as 1–10 ms
  • Built for phones, IoT & industry
  • Enables AR, remote control, VR
  • Supports huge device density
  • Rolling out across the UK

Key Use Cases

Internet of Things (IoT)

Sensors, wearables, and smart appliances stay connected reliably at huge scale

Smart Cities

Traffic lights, energy grids, and public transport share live data to cut congestion

AR & VR

Augmented and virtual experiences stream smoothly with almost no lag

Connected Vehicles

Cars exchange road and safety data in near real time

Industry 4.0

Factories use wireless robots and predictive maintenance sensors

Remote Healthcare

High-quality telemedicine and monitoring for patients at home

Coverage

Real-World Example

Case Study: Smart City Transport Pilot

Before

  • Buses often late with no live updates
  • Traffic sensors on slow 4G links
  • Passengers frustrated by delays
  • Limited data for planners

After 5G

  • Live bus tracking on passenger apps
  • Traffic lights adapt using IoT sensors
  • AR wayfinding at major stations
  • City dashboard for real-time planning

Result

Average wait times down 22%, passenger satisfaction up, and congestion reduced during peak hours.

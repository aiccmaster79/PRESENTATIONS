Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

Why Email Etiquette Matters

Email is often the first way a colleague, tutor or employer judges your professionalism. A badly written email can undo a great piece of work.

Anatomy of a Professional Email

Subject Line

Clear and specific — say what it's about

Greeting

"Hi [Name]," or "Dear [Name],"

Clear Body

Short paragraphs, one purpose per email

Sign-off

"Kind regards," then your name

Attachments

Named clearly, mentioned in the text

Proofread

Check tone, spelling and recipients

To vs CC vs BCC

To

The main recipient — the person who needs to act.

Direct action needed

CC

"Carbon copy" — keeps someone informed. Visible to all recipients.

For awareness, not action

BCC

"Blind carbon copy" — hidden from other recipients.

Group sends, privacy

Practical Task

Rewrite & Practise

Turn three unprofessional emails into professional ones, then practise CC/BCC and safe attachments.

  1. Read three "bad" example emails provided by your tutor
  2. Rewrite each with a clear subject, greeting, body and sign-off
  3. Fix the tone — remove slang, ALL CAPS and vague wording
  4. Send a practice email using CC to loop in a "colleague"
  5. Send a practice group email using BCC to protect addresses
  6. Attach a file safely, naming it clearly and mentioning it in the text
  7. Swap with a partner and check each other's tone and formatting

Test Your Knowledge!

Let's see what you've learned about email etiquette

Q1: BCC should be used when…

AYou want everyone to see all recipients
BYou want to hide the recipient list from other recipients
CYou want to attach a large file
DNever — it should not be used
Answer: B — BCC keeps every recipient's address private from the others.

Q2: A good subject line should be…

ALeft blank
BClear and specific about the email's purpose
CWritten in ALL CAPS
DA joke unrelated to the content
Answer: B — A specific subject line helps the reader prioritise and find the email later.

Q3: Before opening an unexpected attachment you should…

AOpen it immediately
BVerify the sender and scan it first
CForward it to everyone you know
DReply with your password to "confirm" it
Answer: B — Unexpected attachments are a common malware and phishing method.

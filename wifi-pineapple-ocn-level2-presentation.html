Classroom rule

We study rogue access point techniques to understand and defend against wireless attacks — any hands-on testing stays inside an isolated lab network you own or have explicit permission to test.

What is a WiFi Pineapple?

Definition

The WiFi Pineapple is a purpose-built wireless penetration testing platform. It can scan for nearby networks, stand up rogue access points, and capture traffic from devices that connect to it — all managed through a web dashboard.

Think of it like this...

Your phone remembers Wi-Fi networks it has joined before and often asks "is anyone called 'Home-WiFi' nearby?" The Pineapple listens for those requests and can answer "yes, that's me" — tricking the device into connecting.

How the Attack Class Works

Client Probes for Known SSID

Pineapple Responds "That's Me"

Device Auto-Connects & Traffic Flows Through It

This is often called a "Karma" or known-beacon attack, exploiting how devices auto-reconnect to remembered Wi-Fi names.

Legitimate Value vs Misuse Harms

Legitimate Value

  • Finds weak Wi-Fi habits before attackers do
  • Tests captive-portal phishing resilience
  • Validates wireless intrusion detection
  • Supports evidence-based security reports

Misuse Harms

  • Unauthorised interception is a serious crime
  • Can capture private data without consent
  • Deauth attacks disrupt others' legitimate networks
  • Destroys trust and career prospects if misused

Defensive Takeaways

Use WPA3 & PMF

Protected Management Frames (802.11w) reduce deauth attack impact

Disable auto-connect

Turn off "join known networks automatically" on mobile devices

Always-on VPN

Encrypts traffic even if a device joins a rogue access point

Wireless intrusion detection

WIDS/WIPS can flag unexpected access points and deauth floods

Policy on public Wi-Fi

Corporate policy discouraging untrusted networks for work devices

User awareness

Train staff to check certificates and avoid entering credentials on portals

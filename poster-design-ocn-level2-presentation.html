The Design Brief

Audience

Who is this poster actually for?

Message

What's the one thing they must remember?

Purpose

Inform, sell, invite, or warn?

Constraints

Size, format, deadline, print or digital

Brand / Theme

Colours and tone that fit the event

Key Details

Date, time, place, call to action

Colour & Layout Basics

Contrast

Light text on dark backgrounds (or vice versa) so it's readable.

Readability first

Complementary Colours

Opposite colours on the wheel create energy and pop.

Use sparingly for impact

Hierarchy

Biggest / boldest element is read first — headline, then image, then details.

Guide the eye on purpose

Practical Task

Design an Event Poster

Follow a short design brief to produce a poster, then take part in structured peer feedback.

  1. Read the brief: decide your audience and one key message
  2. Sketch two thumbnail layouts on paper first
  3. Choose 2–3 colours and check they contrast well
  4. Build the poster in Canva / Publisher / Slides (or similar)
  5. Arrange headline, image and details using clear hierarchy
  6. Swap with a partner and give "two stars, one wish" feedback
  7. Make one improvement based on the feedback you received

Test Your Knowledge!

Let's see what you've learned about poster design

Q1: What should you decide first when starting a design brief?

AThe font size
BYour audience and key message
CThe printer settings
DThe file name
Answer: B — Everything else follows from knowing who it's for and what they need to know.

Q2: Good layout hierarchy means…

AEvery element is the same size
BThe most important information stands out first
CText is tiny to fit more in
DNo images are allowed
Answer: B — Hierarchy guides the eye to the headline first, then supporting details.

Q3: Useful peer feedback should be…

AVague and unhelpful
BSpecific, kind and actionable
COnly negative
DGiven after the deadline
Answer: B — Specific, actionable feedback is what actually improves the next draft.

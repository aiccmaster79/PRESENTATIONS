What is Blockchain?

Definition

A blockchain is a shared digital ledger that records transactions in linked blocks. Copies are stored across many computers, so no single person controls the whole record.

Think of it like this...

Imagine a shared notebook that everyone can read. When a page is written and sealed, nobody can quietly rewrite it—everyone would notice the change.

How Blocks & Hashes Work

Each block contains:

  • Data — the transactions or records
  • Hash — a unique digital fingerprint of this block
  • Previous hash — links to the block before it
  • Change one block → its hash breaks → the chain no longer matches

Hashes Explained

A hash is a fixed-length code created from any input. Tiny changes to the input create a completely different hash—making tampering easy to spot.

Pay Alice £10a3f9c2...Same input → same hash every time

Key Features

Decentralised

No Single Boss

Many computers (nodes) hold copies. There is no one central database to hack or shut down.

Peer-to-peer network

Immutable

Hard to Change

Once confirmed, records are extremely difficult to alter without everyone noticing.

Linked hashes protect history

Transparent

Visible & Verifiable

Participants can check the ledger. Trust comes from maths and consensus, not one company.

Shared audit trail

Uses Beyond Cryptocurrency

Supply Chains

Track food, medicines, and parts from origin to customer

Smart Contracts

Agreements that run automatically when conditions are met

Digital Identity

Secure credentials students and citizens can control

Voting Systems

Transparent, harder-to-tamper election records (pilots)

Certificates

Verify diplomas and product authenticity online

Healthcare Records

Share trusted medical data between authorised providers

Pros & Cons

Advantages

  • Highly resistant to tampering
  • No single point of failure
  • Transparent audit trails
  • Can cut out middlemen
  • Builds trust between strangers
  • Useful for tracking & verification

Disadvantages

  • Can use significant energy (some networks)
  • Slower than traditional databases
  • Scalability challenges
  • Complex to design and govern
  • Mistakes are hard to reverse
  • Regulatory uncertainty in places

Real-World Example

Case Study: Food Traceability

Challenge

  • Hard to prove where food came from
  • Recalls slow and expensive
  • Paper records easy to lose or fake
  • Customers demand transparency

Blockchain Solution

  • Each farm & warehouse logs shipments
  • Hashes lock the supply history
  • Retailers scan codes to verify origin
  • Recalls target exact batches fast

Result

Trace time cut from days to seconds, fewer wasted products in recalls, and stronger customer trust in food safety.

Robots & Automation

Which Jobs Change?

OCN NI Level 2 Diploma in Information Technology

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Identify where automation and robots are used in workplaces today
  • Compare jobs that automation changes against new jobs it creates
  • Evaluate the business impact of automating a process
  • Redesign a familiar job around automation
  • Predict the new human roles automation is likely to create

Where Automation Works Today

Warehouses

Robotic picking, sorting and stock movement

Checkouts

Self-service scanning and payment

Manufacturing

Assembly-line robots and quality checks

Logistics & Delivery

Route planning and driver-assist systems

Customer Service

Chatbots handling routine queries

Agriculture

Automated harvesting and crop monitoring

Types of Automation

Physical Robots

Machines that move, lift, sort or assemble in the real world

Warehouse arms, factory lines

Software Automation (RPA)

Software robots that repeat digital tasks like data entry

Invoice processing, form-filling

AI-Driven Automation

Systems that make decisions, not just repeat fixed steps

Fraud checks, demand forecasting

Jobs That Change vs Jobs That Are Created

Jobs at Risk of Change

  • Repetitive checkout and cashier roles
  • Manual data entry and basic admin
  • Routine warehouse picking
  • Simple, scripted customer support

New Jobs Created

  • Robot maintenance and technicians
  • Automation/process designers
  • AI trainers and data labellers
  • Customer experience specialists for complex cases

The Business Impact

Efficiency

Tasks completed faster and around the clock

Safety

Robots take on dangerous or repetitive strain tasks

High Setup Cost

Buying and maintaining automation is expensive upfront

Workforce Disruption

Some roles shrink, requiring retraining and support

Practical Task

Redesign a Job Around Automation

In teams, take a familiar job and rebuild it around automation — then predict what comes next.

  1. Choose a familiar job: checkout assistant, warehouse picker, or delivery driver
  2. List the tasks in that job today and mark which ones automation could take over
  3. Redesign the job: what does the human do once automation handles the repetitive parts?
  4. Predict 2-3 new jobs this automation would create (maintenance, oversight, design)
  5. Present your redesigned job and new roles to the class

Kit Needed

None required — a discussion and poster/whiteboard activity.

Test Your Knowledge!

Let's see what you've learned about robots and automation

Q1: Which of these is an example of automation in a warehouse?

AA robotic arm that picks and sorts stock
BA pencil and paper stock list
CA handwritten delivery note
DA landline telephone
Answer: A — Robotic picking and sorting arms are a common real-world example of physical automation in warehouses.

Q2: What is RPA (software automation) mainly used for?

ABuilding physical robots only
BRepeating routine digital tasks like data entry or invoice processing
CCooking food
DReplacing all creative work
Answer: B — RPA (Robotic Process Automation) automates repetitive digital/software tasks, not physical work.

Q3: Which best describes the overall effect of automation on jobs?

AIt only ever destroys jobs with nothing created in return
BIt changes some roles while also creating new ones like maintenance and oversight
CIt has no effect on the world of work at all
DIt only affects office jobs, never physical ones
Answer: B — Automation typically changes the mix of jobs available — reducing some repetitive roles while creating new technical and oversight roles.

Key Takeaways

Automation Is Widespread

From warehouses to customer service, it's already in use

Jobs Shift, Not Just Vanish

Some roles shrink while new technical roles appear

Business Impact Is Mixed

Efficiency gains come with upfront cost and disruption

Redesign, Don't Just Remove

The best outcomes redesign jobs around what humans do best
